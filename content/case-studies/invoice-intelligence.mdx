## Context

The client received invoices from a long tail of vendors, each with its own layout,
terminology, and quirks. Their existing process was manual: a small team rekeyed line
items into the accounting system by hand. That approach capped throughput, introduced
transcription errors, and made month-end reconciliation slow.

The goal was a system that could ingest an invoice in whatever form it arrived and return
structured, validated data the downstream accounting workflow could trust without a human
re-checking every field.

## The core problem

Document extraction is easy to demo and hard to ship. A prototype that handles the ten
invoices you tested with says little about the hundredth vendor format you have never seen.
Two failure modes dominated:

1. Layout drift. Vendors change templates, add surcharges, or restructure line items.
2. Silent wrongness. A plausible-looking number in the wrong field is worse than an obvious
   blank, because it passes review and corrupts the books.

The system had to be resilient to the first and loud about the second.

## Approach

I built the pipeline around LLM-driven OCR feeding a structured-output stage constrained by
explicit schemas. Rather than parsing each vendor layout with bespoke rules, the model reads
the document and emits data shaped to a fixed contract.

<Callout title="Design principle">
Validation is the product. The extraction model proposes; the schema disposes. Every field
that reaches the accounting system has passed a typed, business-rule-aware check, and anything
that fails is routed to review instead of written silently.
</Callout>

### Schema-constrained extraction

Each invoice is resolved against a Pydantic schema describing the fields the business actually
uses: vendor identity, line items, quantities, unit prices, totals, tax, and dates. The schema
encodes business rules directly, so an extraction where line items do not sum to the stated
total is rejected at the boundary rather than discovered three steps later.

### Handling the long tail

Because the model works from the document's content rather than a per-vendor template, adding a
new vendor format usually costs nothing. The system absorbed new layouts as they appeared
without code changes for each one, which is what kept it viable across more than a hundred
formats.

## Results

The figures below are representative of the deployed system's steady-state operation.

- Roughly 3,000 invoices processed per month with no per-vendor configuration.
- More than 100 distinct vendor layouts handled by a single extraction path.
- Manual rekeying reduced to an exception-handling queue for the small fraction of documents
  that fail validation.

The team's time shifted from transcription to reviewing the handful of flagged edge cases,
which is where human judgment actually adds value.

## What I would carry forward

The lesson that generalized best: treat the model's output as a proposal and put the
correctness burden on a validation layer you fully control. The schema is the contract, the
review queue is the safety valve, and the model is free to be merely very good rather than
perfect.
